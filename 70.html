The 70's

The Birth of Disco

Dance music of the seventies is heavily influenced by the big soul and jazz orchestras of the late sixties and early seventies. Like many music groups from other genres like Jazz Big Band or classical music, they consisted of many personnel, playing many different, largely traditional instruments. It was quite common for the groups to have a separate string section and a horn section in addition to a rhythm section. For a musical genre that is sometimes considered quite straightforward and “easy”, its origins and early sound are far from simple!

Many consider MFSB (for “Mother, Father, Sister, Brother”, a group of 45 musicians working with famous producers Kenneth Gamble and Leon Huff on the legendary “Sound of Philadelphia”) to be the first real Dance orchestra. They were followed by groups like Rose Royce, Kool and the Gang, Love Unlimited Orchestra. The orchestras started including African influences into their music – a style that put bass guitars and rhythmic instrumentation centre stage in their compositions.

In 1972, Cameroon artist Manu Dibango published his landmark track “Soul Makossa”. Composed around two bass guitars and two drum kits - and therefore focusing on a driving rhythm - it is seen as the first ever Disco track. After Earl Young, drummer for MFSB invented the “four-on-the-floor” beat, the basis of the since then legendary dance movement called DISCO was laid. It is the origin after which essentially all dance music that came afterward is modelled.

Electronic Music

On a separate track in the seventies, developments in computing capabilities and electronic equipment gave way to a new generation of musicians, who introduced electronic and computerized gear into music, sometimes fully replacing traditional instruments. One of the first musical acts to favour the use of synthesizers, sequencers, drum computers, vocoders - and even creative self-built electronic gadgets - over guitars, horns and violins was Kraftwerk from Germany. The music they created forms a blueprint for many electronic music produced after, even today manifesting in techno, electro, and synth-pop.

Although the electronic music they made is sometimes described as sounding “detached”, “clinical” or “alien”, other musicians in the seventies quickly followed their lead. People like Jon and Vangelis, Frenchman Jean-Michel Jarre, Gary Numan (under various names, f.i Tubeway Army) and groups like OMD Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark and Depeche Mode focused on electronic equipment to make music. While most of their tracks are not generally considered "danceable", the influence they had on dance music is unmistakable.

Interestingly, electronic music mostly originated in Europe and United Kingdom, while Disco and dance in the sixties and seventies was born in the United States.

There is no other musical genre that has embraced and incorporated electronic music and computer equipment as much as dance music has. The convergence between the two becomes abundantly clear in the eighties!
